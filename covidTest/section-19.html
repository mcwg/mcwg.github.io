Subsection 5.6.2 Looking at \(N\) plotted against time

All our previous explorations of the logistic model relied on graphs that had \(P\) on the horizontal axis. However, in practice the values for \(N\) are reported daily or weekly so it is much more natural to consider the graph of \(N\) plotted against time. In fact almost all the graphs we see in the media or data sites are of \(P\) or \(N\) plotted against time. Figure \ref{f:Nvtexample} is one such example.

We know we can construct the function \(P\) from the given \(N\) data, but it would be much easier if we could recognize logistic growth from the more readily available time graphs. It turns out that \(N\) plotted against time can also be used to determine if \(P\) grows logistically.

To understand this further we need to solve the logistic differential equation to get a formula for \(P\) in terms of \(t\text{.}\) We can then take its derivative to get an explicit formula for \(N\) in terms of \(t\text{.}\) We study the properties of \(N\) directly from this formula.

Using the method of separation of variables and partial fraction expansions we can show that the solution to

\begin{equation*} dP/dt=kP(L-P)\qquad P(0)=P_0 \end{equation*}

is given by

\begin{equation*} P(t)=\frac{\left(\frac{P_0}{L-P_0}\right)Le^{kt}}{1+\left(\frac{P_0}{L-P_0}\right)e^{kt}}. \end{equation*}

We can simplify this expression by setting \(A=\frac{P_0}{L-P_0}\) and we have

\begin{equation*} P(t)=\frac{ALe^{kt}}{1+Ae^{kt}}\qquad\text{and}\qquad dP/dt=\frac{LAke^{kt}}{\left(e^{kt}+A\right)^2}. \end{equation*}
Edit comment: Warning.

The definition of \(A\) is backwards here (in relation to the textbook). Should be:

\begin{equation*} A=\frac{L-P_0}{P_0} \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} P(t)=\frac{L}{1+Ae^{-kt}} \end{equation*}

This needs to be adjusted.

Figure Figure 5.6.1 shows the graphs of \(P\) and \(N=dP/dt\) for parameter values \(A=k=1\) and \(L=10\text{.}\) As expected, the graph of \(P\) has a horizontal asymptote at \(P=0\) and at \(P=10\) and the maximum value of \(N\) occurs at the inflection point of the graph of \(P\text{,}\) which occurs at the point \((0,5)\text{.}\)

The new features of \(N\) that we can see in the time graph is that its graph is a bell shaped curve. It is an even function, namely it is symmetric about the vertical axis. You will now have a chance to explore which of the features of this graph came about because of our choice of parameter values, and which ones are always present when \(N\) is the derivative of a logistic curve.
