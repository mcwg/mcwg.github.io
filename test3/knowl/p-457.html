Choice (b) is more manageable.

  1. Given \(u=e^x\text{,}\) \(v'=x^2\) we have \(u'=e^x\text{,}\) \(v=(1/3)x^3\text{,}\) so

    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int\underbrace{e^x}_{u}\underbrace{x^2}_{v'}\,dx\amp=\underbrace{e^x}_{u}\underbrace{(1/3)x^3}_{v}-\int\underbrace{e^x}_{u'}\underbrace{(1/3)x^3}_{v}\,dx \\ \text{so}\quad\int x^2e^x\,dx\amp=\frac13x^3e^x-\int\frac13x^3e^x\,dx. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

    Notice that the integral on the right is less manageable than the integral we started with.

  2. Given \(u=x^2\text{,}\) \(v'=e^x\) we have \(u'=2x\text{,}\) \(v=e^x\text{,}\) so

    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \int\underbrace{x^2}_{u}\underbrace{e^x}_{v'}\,dx\amp=\underbrace{x^2}_{u}\underbrace{e^x}_{v}-\int\underbrace{2x}_{u'}\underbrace{e^x}_{v}\,dx \\ \text{so}\quad\int x^2e^x\,dx\amp=x^2e^x-2\int xe^x\,dx. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

    Notice that, since we have seen how to find \(\displaystyle \int xe^{x}\,dx,\) the integral on the right is more manageable than the integral we started with.
