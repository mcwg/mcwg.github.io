Section 1 Recognizing an Integrand as the Derivative of a Product

We can use the product rule to find certain integrals. For instance, according to the product rule,

\begin{equation*} e^x+xe^x=\frac{d}{dx}(xe^x). \end{equation*}

By the Fundamental Theorem, this means that \(e^x+xe^x\) can be integrated:

\begin{equation*} \int(e^x+xe^x)\,dx=xe^x+C. \end{equation*}

Problem 1.2.

Sometimes the product rule lets us find a sum of integrals. For instance,

\begin{align*} \int e^x\,dx+\int xe^x\,dx\amp = xe^x+C \\ \int\left(e^x+xe^x\right)\,dx\amp = xe^x+C \amp \text{(combining the sum)} \\ e^x+xe^x\amp = \frac d{dx}\left(xe^x\right) \amp \text{(by the product rule)} \end{align*}
