Section 2 Using the Product Rule to Evaluate a Less Familiar Integral from a Known Integral

The product rule can help us to evaluate a sum of integrals, such as

\begin{equation*} \int e^x\,dx+\int xe^x\,dx=xe^x+C. \end{equation*}

We may recognize one of the integrals in our sum, like \(\displaystyle \int e^x\,dx,\) while the other one may be less familiar, like \(\displaystyle \int xe^x\,dx.\)

This lets us write a formula for the less familiar integral:

\begin{align*} \underbrace{\int e^x\,dx}_{\text{more familiar}}+\underbrace{\int xe^x\,dx}_{\text{less familiar}}\amp =xe^x+C\\ \underbrace{\int xe^x\,dx}_{\text{less familiar}}\amp =xe^x-\underbrace{\int e^x\,dx}_{\text{more familiar}} \\ \amp =xe^x-e^x+C. \end{align*}

Now we have a formula for the less familiar integral:

\begin{equation*} \int xe^x\,dx=xe^x-e^x+C. \end{equation*}
