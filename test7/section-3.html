Section 3 First set: Getting the definitions straight

It usually is a mistake to try to memorize the definition of logs without any understanding of what is going on. The symbols “jump around” too much from one equation to the other in

\begin{equation*} y=\log x \quad\text{ means }\quad 10^y=x \end{equation*}

for pure memory to not fail you in the future (specially since \(10^y=x\) can equally well be written in the form \(x=10^y\)).

Probably the best way to keep all of this straight in the long run is to keep in mind the definition in terms of inverse functions:

\(\log x\) is the inverse function of \(10^x\), which says that \(10^\Box\) and \(\log \Box\) undo each other:

\begin{equation*} 10^{\log x}=x \quad\text{and}\quad \log 10^x=x \end{equation*}

In other words, log of a number is whatever goes in the box to give you the number back in \(10^{\square}=\text{number}\text{,}\) or

\begin{equation*} 10^{\text{log of number}}=\text{number}. \end{equation*}

The same happens with the natural log \(\ln\text{:}\)

\(\ln x\) is the inverse function to \(e^x\).

The \(\log\) notation in different systems.

Try searching for log 10 in both Google and in Wolfram Alpha separately. What do you notice?

It is important to always keep in mind that some systems use the notation \(\log =\ln\text{.}\) A quick way to test if this is the case is to compute \(\log 10\text{.}\) If you get \(1\text{,}\) then \(\log\) is the base-ten log as defined above. If you get \(2.30255...\) then \(\log\) is the natural log in your system!

In all the exercises below you can use log to input the base-ten log.
