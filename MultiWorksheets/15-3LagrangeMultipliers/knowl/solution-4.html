Solution 5.2.1.

  1. We use Lagrange multipliers with constraint function \(g(x,y)=x^2+y^2\text{.}\) We know that \(f(x,y)\) has a global maximum and minimum subject to the constraint because the constraint is closed and bounded. We have \(\nabla f={5}y\vec{i} +{5}x\vec{j}\text{,}\) \(\nabla g=2x\vec{i} +2y\vec{j}\text{,}\) and we are trying to solve \(\nabla f=\lambda \nabla g\text{.}\) Resolving components, this means we are trying to solve the equations

    \begin{equation*} {5}y=\lambda (2x), \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} {5}x=\lambda (2y) \end{equation*}

    and

    \begin{equation*} x^2+y^2=1. \end{equation*}

    For the first equation, since \({5}y=\lambda (2x)\text{,}\) we have \(y=\lambda (2x/{5})\text{.}\) Substituting into the second equation, we get \({5}x=\lambda (2 \lambda (2x/{5}))\) or \({25}x=\lambda^2 (4x)\text{.}\) This means either \(x=0\) or \(\lambda^2={25}/4\text{.}\) We look at these two cases individually.

    If \(x=0\text{,}\) then since \(y=\lambda (2x/{5})\text{,}\) we also have \(y=0\) giving the point \((0,0)\) However, this point does not satisfy the constraint \(x^2+y^2=1\text{.}\)

    If \(\lambda^2={25}/4\text{,}\) then \(\lambda =\pm {5}/2\text{.}\) If \(\lambda ={5}/2\text{,}\) then since \({5}y=\lambda (2x)\) we have \({5}y={5}x\) or \(x=y\text{.}\) Substituting \(y=x\) into the constraint, we get \(2x^2=1\text{,}\) or \(x=\pm 1/\sqrt{2}\text{.}\) This gives two points, \((1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2})\) and \((-1/\sqrt{2}, -1/\sqrt{2})\text{.}\) If instead \(\lambda =-{5}/2\text{,}\) using similar reasoning, we get the points \((-1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2})\) and \((1/\sqrt{2}, -1/\sqrt{2})\text{.}\)

    Thus there are four points where the minimum and maximum can occur. Plugging each point into \(f(x,y)\text{,}\) we get

    \begin{equation*} f(1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2})={5}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) +{6}={17}/2 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(-1/\sqrt{2}, -1/\sqrt{2})={5}\left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) +{6}={17}/2 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(-1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2})={5}\left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) +{6}={7}/2 \end{equation*}
    \begin{equation*} f(1/\sqrt{2}, -1/\sqrt{2})={5}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \left(-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\right) +{6}={7}/2. \end{equation*}

    Therefore both \((1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2})\) and \((-1/\sqrt{2}, -1/\sqrt{2})\) are maxima with a value of \({17}/2\) and both \((-1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2})\) and \((1/\sqrt{2}, -1/\sqrt{2})\) are minima with a value of \({7}/2\text{.}\)

  2. The critical points of \(f\) occur where \(\nabla f =\vec{0}\) or is undefined. Since \(\nabla f={5}y\vec{i} +{5}x\vec{j}\) it is never undefined, and is equal to \(\vec{0}\) only at the point \((0,0)\text{.}\)

  3. We know that \(f(x,y)\) has a global maximum and minimum subject to the constraint because the constraint is closed and bounded. A global minimum or maximum of \(f\) subject to the constraint \(x^2+y^2\leq 1\) will either be a minimum or maximum on the boundary \(x^2+y^2=1\) and can therefore be found using Lagrange multipliers, or will occur at a critical point on the inside \(\newcommand{\lt}{\lt }x^2+y^2\lt 1\text{.}\) We already found the global minimum and maximum on the boundary in (a), so we just need to compare to the value at the critical points.

    From (b), the only critical point is \((0,0)\text{,}\) which does occur inside the boundary. However, since \(f(0,0)={6}\) is not bigger than the global maximum on the boundary and not smaller than the global minimum on the boundary, the maximum and minimum values subject to the constraint \(x^2+y^2\le 1\) are the same as the ones subject to the constaint \(x^2+y^2=1\text{.}\)
