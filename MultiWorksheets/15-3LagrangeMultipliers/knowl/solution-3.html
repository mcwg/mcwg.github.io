Solution 5.1.1.

We use Lagrange multipliers with constraint function \(g(x,y)=x^2+y^2\text{.}\) We have \(\nabla f=2x\vec{i} +({4}-2y)\vec{j}\text{,}\) \(\nabla g=2x\vec{i} +2y\vec{j}\text{,}\) and we are trying to solve \(\nabla f=\lambda \nabla g\text{.}\) Resolving components, this means we are trying to solve the equations

\begin{equation*} 2x=\lambda (2x), \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} ({4}-2y)=\lambda (2y) \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} x^2+y^2={16}. \end{equation*}

For the first equation, since \(2x=\lambda (2x)\text{,}\) we must have either \(\lambda =1\) or \(x=0\text{,}\) so we consider these two cases separately.

If \(x=0\text{,}\) then the equation \(x^2+y^2={16}\) gives \(y^2={16}\) or \(y=\pm {4}\text{.}\) This gives the points \((0,{4})\) and \((0,-{4})\text{.}\)

If \(\lambda =1\text{,}\) then the equation \(({4}-2y)=\lambda (2y)\) becomes \(({4}-2y)=2y\) or \(4y={4}\text{,}\) so \(y={1}\text{.}\) Plugging into the equation \(x^2+y^2={16}\text{,}\) this gives \(x^2={15}\) or \(x=\pm \sqrt{{15}}\text{.}\) This gives the points \((\sqrt{{15}},{1})\) and \((-\sqrt{{15}},{1})\text{.}\)

Thus there are four points where the minimum and maximum can occur. Plugging each point into \(f(x,y)\text{,}\) we get

\begin{equation*} \begin{align} f(0,{4})\amp =-{16}+{16}={0}\text{,}\\ f(0,-{4})\amp =-{16}-{16}={-32}\text{,}\\ f(\sqrt{{15}},{1})\amp ={15}-{1}+{4}={18}\text{,}\\ f(-\sqrt{{15}},{1})\amp ={15}-{1}+{4}={18}\text{.} \end{align} \end{equation*}

Therefore both \((\sqrt{{15}},{1})\) and \((-\sqrt{{15}},{1})\) are global maxima with a value of \({18}\) and \((0,-{4})\) is a global minimum with a value of \({-32}\text{.}\)
