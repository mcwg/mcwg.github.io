Solution 4.5.1.

Since the component functions of \(\vec{F} =y\vec{i} +x \vec{j}+z\vec{k}\) are defined everywhere, \(\vec{F}\) does not have any holes in its domain, so the curl test applies. We have \(\text{curl}\vec{F} =(0-0)\vec{i} -(0-0)\vec{j} +(1-1)\vec{k}=\vec{0}\text{,}\) so \(\vec{F}\) is conservative. Integrating and combining, we get \(f(x,y)=xy +z^2/2\) as a potential function. Since we already know the endpoints of \(C\text{,}\) we apply the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus getting

\begin{equation*} \newcommand{\amp}{\amp }\begin{aligned} \int_{C} (y\vec{i} +x \vec{j}+z\vec{k})\cdot d\vec{r} \amp =f({1},{1},{2})-f({0},{-1},{2}) \\ \amp = {3} -({2}).\\ \amp = {1} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
