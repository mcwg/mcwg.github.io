The component functions of \(\vec{F} =(1/(x+y+{4}))\vec{i} +(1/(x+y+{4})+y))\vec{j}\) are defined everywhere except \(y=-x-{4}\text{.}\) The curve \(y=e^x\) from \(x=0\) to \(x={3}\) appears entirely in the first quadrant, which is a simply connected region where \(\vec{F}\) is defined. It follows that we can test whether \(\vec{F}\) is conservative in the first quadrant by applying the curl test. We have

\begin{equation*} \text{scalar curl of }\vec{F} =-\frac{1}{(x+y+{4})^2} -\left(-\frac{1}{(x+y+{4})^2}\right) =0 \end{equation*}

so \(\vec{F}\) is conservative in the first quadrant. Integrating and combining, we get \(f(x,y)=\ln{(x+y+{4})} +y^2/2\) as a potential function. Since the curve is \(y=e^x\) from \(x=0\) to \(x={3}\text{,}\) the endpoints of \(C\) are \((0,1)\) and \(({3},e^{3})\text{,}\) so applying the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, we get

\begin{equation*} \newcommand{\amp}{\amp }\begin{aligned} \amp \int_{C} \left( \frac{1}{x+y+{4}} \vec{i} +\left( \frac{1}{x+y+{4}} +y\right)\vec{j} \right) \cdot d\vec{r} \\ \amp =f({3},e^{3})-f(0,1) \\ \amp =\left(\ln{({7}+e^{3})} +\frac{e^{6}}{2} \right) -\ln 2- \frac{1}{2} \\ \amp=\ln{({7}+e^{3})} +\frac{e^{6}-1}{2} -\ln 2 \end{aligned} \end{equation*}
