Solution 4.2.1.

Since the component functions of \(\vec{F} ={4}xy\vec{i} +{2}x^2\vec{j}\) are defined everywhere, \(\vec{F}\) does not have any holes in its domain, so the curl test applies. The scalar curl of \(\vec{F}\) is \({4}x-{4}x=0\text{,}\) so \(\vec{F}\) is conservative. Though we could find a potential function, in this case since \(C\) is closed, its endpoints coincide, so by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, we get

\begin{equation*} \int_{C} ({4}xy\vec{i} +{2}x^2\vec{j})\cdot d\vec{r} =0. \end{equation*}

Note that you could also get the value by parameterizing the curve as \(\vec r (t)=\cos(\theta)\vec{i}+\sin(\theta)\vec{j}\) with \(0\leq \theta \le 2\pi\text{,}\) but it gives a long computation

\begin{equation*} \int_{C} ({4}xy\vec{i} +{2}x^2\vec{j})\cdot d\vec{r}=\int_0^{2\pi} \left({2} \cos^3\theta - {4} \sin^2 \theta \cos \theta \right) \, d\theta = 0 \end{equation*}
