A critical point of a function \(h(x,y)\) is a point where \(\nabla h(x,y)=\vec{0}\) or where \(\nabla h(x,y)\) is undefined. The partial derivatives

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} h_x(x,y)\amp= {24} x^2 + {28} x + y^2 \\ h_y(x,y)\amp= 2xy+2y, \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

show that \(\nabla h(x,y)\) is defined everywhere, and \(\nabla h(x,y)=\vec{0}\) only at the points which are solutions to the system of equations

\begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} {24} x^2 + {28} x + y^2 \amp= 0 \\ 2xy+2y \amp = 0. \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

Factoring out a \(2y\) in the second equation gives \(2y(1+x)=0\text{,}\) so either \(2y=0\) or \(1+x=0\) (or both). This shows that any solution \((x,y)\) to the system must have \(y=0\) or \(x=-1\text{.}\) Setting \(y=0\) in the first equation gives \[28x+24x^2=0,\] which has solutions \(x=0\) or \(x={-{\textstyle\frac{7}{6}}}\text{.}\) Thus, \((x,y)=(0,0)\) and \((x,y)=({-{\textstyle\frac{7}{6}}},0)\) are two solutions (the only solutions with \(y=0\)). Setting \(x=-1\) in the first equation gives \(-4+y^2=0\text{,}\) so \(y=\pm 2\text{.}\) Thus, \((x,y)=(-1,-2)\) and \((x,y)=(-1,2)\) are two more solutions (the only solutions with \(x=-1\)).

Therefore, the solutions to the system are \((0,0)\text{,}\) \(({-{\textstyle\frac{7}{6}}},0)\text{,}\) \((-1,-2)\) and \((-1,2)\text{.}\) These are the critical points.
