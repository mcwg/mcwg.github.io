A critical point of a function \(g(x,y)\) is a point where \(\nabla g(x,y)=\vec{0}\) or where \(\nabla g(x,y)\) is undefined. The partial derivatives

\begin{align*} g_x(x,y)\amp = x+y+ \\ g_y(x,y)\amp = x-y-, \end{align*}

show that \(\nabla g(x,y)\) is defined everywhere, and \(\nabla g(x,y)=\vec{0}\) only at the points which are solutions to the system of equations

\begin{align*} x+y+ \amp = 0 \\ x-y- \amp = 0. \end{align*}

If we add these two equations together, the \(y\) will cancel giving \(x=0\text{,}\) so \(x=0\text{.}\) Setting \(x=0\) in either of the two equations gives \(y=\text{.}\) Thus, the only solution to the system is \((x,y)=(0,)\text{.}\) Thus, the only critical point of \(g\) is \((0,)\text{.}\)

We compute

\begin{equation*} D=\det \pmatrix{g_{xx} \amp g_{xy} \\ g_{yx} \amp g_{yy}}=\det \pmatrix{ \amp \\ \amp -}=-, \end{equation*}

and since \(D\lt 0\) (at any point so in particular at \((0,)\)), we see that \((0,)\) is a saddle point.
