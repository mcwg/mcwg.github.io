Solution 5.4.1.

To determine whether it is continuous or differentiable, we need to determine whether the component functions are. The \(\vec{j}\) component function \(t^2-t\text{,}\) is continuous and differentiable everywhere. The \(\vec{i}\) component function is continuous and differentiable everywhere except possibly the point where the two pieces meet, when \(t=1\text{,}\) so we consider this point in detail.

Since \(g(1)={6}\cdot 1+1={7}\) and \(\lim_{t\rightarrow 1^-} g(t)=(1)^2+{6}={7}\text{,}\) the left and right hand sides of the curve match up at \(t=1\text{,}\) so \(g(t)\) and hence \(\vec{r}(t)\) is continuous at \(t=1\) and hence on the entire interval \([-1,4]\text{.}\) The slope on the left hand side of \(t=1\) can be found finding the derivative of the left-hand piecewise function \(h(t)=t^2+{6}\) when \(t=1\text{.}\) In particular, the slope of the left side will be \(h'(1)=2\text{.}\) On the other hand, the slope of the right hand side can be found finding the derivative of the right-hand piecewise function \(f(t)={6}t+1\) when \(t=1\text{.}\) In particular, the slope of the left side will be \(f'(1)={6}\text{.}\) Since these two slopes do not match up, \(g(t)\) is not differentiable at \(t=1\text{,}\) and hence neither is \(\vec{r}(t)\text{.}\)
