Solution 4.6.1.

In order to write the equation for \(\Pi\text{,}\) we need a point on \(\Pi\) and we need a normal vector. Since the line \(\vec{r}(t)\) passes through the plane \(\Pi\) at \(t=-1\text{,}\) the point

\begin{equation*} \begin{gathered} \vec{r}(-1)=(2+{6}\cdot(-1))\vec{i} +(2-{1}\cdot (-1))\vec{j} +(3-{3}\cdot (-1))\vec{k}\\ ={-4\mathit{\vec i}+3\mathit{\vec j}+6\mathit{\vec k}}\\ ={\left(-4,3,6\right)} \end{gathered} \end{equation*}

is on \(\Pi\text{.}\)

Next, rewriting, \(\vec{r}(t)\) in the form \(\vec{P} +\vec{v}t\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} \begin{gathered} \vec{r}(t) ={\left(2+6t\right)\mathit{\vec i}+\left(2-t\right)\mathit{\vec j}+\left(3-3t\right)\mathit{\vec k}}\\ =2\vec{i} +2\vec{j} +3\vec{k} +({6\mathit{\vec i}-\mathit{\vec j}-3\mathit{\vec k}})t \end{gathered} \end{equation*}

Therefore, the vector \(\vec{v} ={6\mathit{\vec i}-\mathit{\vec j}-3\mathit{\vec k}}\) is a direction vector for \(\vec{r}(t)\text{.}\) Since \(\vec{r}(t)\) is perpendicular to \(\Pi\text{,}\) this direction vector is perpendicular to \(\vec{r}(t)\) and thus is a normal vector. Therefore, using the standard equation for a plane, we get

\begin{equation*} {6}(x-({-4}))-{1}(y-({3}))-{3}(z-({6}))=0 \end{equation*}

as a scalar equation for \(\Pi\text{.}\) Alternatively, solving for \(z\text{,}\) we get

\begin{equation*} z={2x - {\textstyle\frac{1}{3}}y+15} \end{equation*}
