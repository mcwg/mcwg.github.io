We can determine the nature of a critical point by the behavior of a function just before and just after that point. For example, at a minimum, a function decreases into the point, and increases away from the point.

  • The function \(g\) is increasing on both the interval \((-\infty, 15)\) and \((15, 17)\text{.}\) This means that the critical point \(x=15\) is neither a minimum or maximum.
  • The function \(g\) is increasing on the interval \((15,17)\) and decreasing on \((17, 21)\text{.}\) This means \(g\) decreases into the point \(x=17\) and then increases away from the point \(x=17\) and so \(x=17\) is a minimum.
  • The function \(g\) is decreasing on the interval \((17,21)\) and increasing on \((21, \infty)\text{.}\) This means \(g\) increases into the point \(x=21\) and then decreases away from the point \(x=21\) and so \(x=21\) is a maximum.
