Section 1 Summary

  • The method of integration by substitution reverses the chain rule.

  • To find \(\int f(x)\,dx\) with the substitution \(w=w(x)\text{,}\) transform both the integrand \(f(x)\) and the differential \(dx\text{.}\) For the differential, use

    \begin{equation*} dw = w'(x)dx = (dw/dx)\, dx. \end{equation*}

  • To use substitution to find definite integrals:

    • Compute the indefinite integral, expressing an antiderivative in terms of the original variable, and then evaluate the result at the original limits, or

    • Convert the original limits to new limits in terms of the new variable and do not convert the antiderivative back to the original variable.

Subsection 1.1 The "typical" example

To find the integral

\begin{equation*} \int 2x\sqrt{x^2+1}\, dx \end{equation*}

we recognize that \(2x\) is the derivative of \(w=x^2+1\text{,}\) and so we may be able to write the integral in terms of \(w\) exclusively:

\begin{align*} \int 2x\sqrt{x^2+1}\, dx \amp = \int 2x \sqrt{w}\, dx \amp\amp w=x^2+1\\ \amp = \int \sqrt{w}\, 2x dx \amp\amp dw=2x dx\\ \amp = \int \sqrt{w}\, dw \amp\amp \text{Method worked since this has no}~x~\text{left.}\\ \amp = \frac{w^{3/2}}{3/2}+C\\ \amp = \frac{2}{3}w^{3/2}+C \amp\amp\text{We are done with the}~w~\text{and can go back to the}~x.\\ \amp = \frac{2}{3}(x^2+1)^{3/2}+C \end{align*}
