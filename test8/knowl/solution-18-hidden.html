1. If \(P(t)\) is the investmentâ€™s value after \(t\) years, we have \(P(t)=P_{0}e^{{0.02} t} \text{.}\) We want to find \(t\) such that \(P(t)\) is three times its initial value, \(P_0 \text{.}\) Therefore, we need to solve:

    \begin{equation*} \newcommand{\amp}{\amp }\begin{aligned} P(t) \amp = {3} P_0 \\ P_{0}e^{{0.02} t} \amp = {3} P_0 \\ e^{{0.02} t} \amp = {3} \\ \ln e^{{0.02} t} \amp = \ln {3} \\ {0.02} t \amp = \ln {3} \\ t \amp = (\ln {3} )/{0.02} \approx {54.931} \hbox{ years.} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

    With continuous compounding, the investment should triple in about \(54.9\) years.

  2. If the interest is compounded only once a year, the formula we will use is \(P(t)=P_0b^t\) where \(b\) is the percent value of what the investment had been one year earlier. If it is earning \({2}\%\) interest compounded once a year, it is \(10{2}\%\) of what it had been the previous year, so our formula is \(P(t)=P_{0}({1.02})^{t}.\)

    Using this new formula, we will now solve

    \begin{equation*} \newcommand{\amp}{\amp }\begin{aligned} P(t) \amp = {3} P_0 \\ P_0({1.02})^{t} \amp = {3} P_{0} \\ ({1.02})^{t} \amp = {3} \\ \log ({1.02})^t \amp = \log {3} \\ t \log {1.02} \amp = \log {3} \\ t \amp = \frac{\log {3} }{\log {1.02}}\approx {55.478} \hbox{ years.} \end{aligned} \end{equation*}

    So, compounding once a year, it will take about \(55.5\) years for the investment to triple.
