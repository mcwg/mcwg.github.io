Section 4 Second set: Identifying and interpreting the parameters

Meaning of \(a\).

Setting \(t=0\) gives \(Q=a\) in both forms \(Q=ab^t\) and \(Q=ae^{kt}\text{,}\) so \(a\) has a simple interpretation: \(a\) is the value of \(Q\) when \(t=0\text{.}\)

Meaning of \(b\).

Note that:

\begin{equation*} \text{value of } Q \text{ at } t+1 = ab^{t+1}=ab^t\cdot b = b\times Q, \end{equation*}

so \(b\) is the factor by which \(Q\) gets multiplied in one unit of time. In particular, \(Q\) is growing if \(b\gt 1\) and decaying if \(b\lt 1\text{.}\)

Meaning of \(k\).

Note that:

\begin{equation*} \text{value of } Q \text{ at } t+1 = ae^{k(t+1)}=ae^{kt}\cdot e^k = e^k \times Q, \end{equation*}

so \(k\) is related to the factor of growth, but the relation is not so straight forward as with \(b\text{.}\) Each unit of time corresponds to a change by a factor of \(e^k\text{.}\)

Thus, \(Q=ae^{kt}\) is decaying if \(k\lt 0\) (since \(e^k\lt 1\) if \(k\lt 0\)) and growing if \(k\gt 0\) (since \(e^k\gt 1\) in that case). \(k\) is called the continuous growth rate and is usually given in percent form. For example, \(k=4.3\%\) corresponds to \(Q=e^{0.034t}\text{.}\)

Decay rates.

A key thing to keep in mind is that decay rates are not aways readily visible from the formula. For example \(Q=a(0.93)^t\) corresponds to \(b=0.93\text{.}\) Multiplying by \(0.93\) in one unit of time corresponds to a reduction of \(7\%\) in one unit of time (\(0.93=1-0.07\)), so \(Q=a(0.93)^t\) is decaying at at rate of \(7\%\) per unit of time.

Things are a little bit simpler with the form \(Q=ae^{kt}\text{.}\) For example, \(Q=ae^{-0.03t}\) corresponds to continuous decay at a rate of \(3\%\text{.}\)

We also say the continuous growth rate is \(-3\%\) (negative growth rate is decay).
